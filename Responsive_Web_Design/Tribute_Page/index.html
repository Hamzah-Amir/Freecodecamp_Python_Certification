Quaid-e-Azam Muhammad Ali Jinnah

Quaid-e-Azam Muhammad Ali Jinnah (born Mahomedali Jinnahbhai; 25 December 1876 – 11 September 1948) was a barrister, politician, and the revered founder of Pakistan. Serving as the leader of the All-India Muslim League from 1913 until the creation of Pakistan on 14 August 1947, Jinnah became the country’s first Governor-General, a role he held until his passing. Widely known as the Father of the Nation, his vision, determination, and leadership were instrumental in carving out a separate homeland for the Muslims of the Indian subcontinent.

Born at Wazir Mansion in Karachi—then part of the Bombay Presidency of British India—Jinnah was the eldest of seven children of Jinnahbhai Poonja and Mithibai Jinnah. His family hailed from a Khoja Muslim background with roots in Gujarat, and later followed Twelver Shi’a teachings, although accounts suggest he may have identified as Sunni later in life. Jinnah attended Sindh Madrassatul-Islam and the Christian Missionary Society High School before moving to London at the age of 16. There, he trained as a barrister at Lincoln’s Inn, reputedly choosing it because its entrance displayed the names of history’s greatest lawgivers—including Prophet Muhammad (PBUH).

Barrister and Early Political Career

At the age of 20, Jinnah began practicing law in Bombay, becoming the only Muslim barrister in the city at the time. Initially facing challenges, his career took a turn when he worked in the chambers of John Molesworth MacPherson, the acting Advocate General of Bombay. In 1900, he briefly served as a magistrate before returning to private practice, famously rejecting a lucrative permanent post with the remark that he intended to earn “1,500 rupees a day” — a goal he eventually achieved.

In the early decades of the 20th century, Jinnah was active in the Indian National Congress, advocating for Hindu-Muslim unity and playing a key role in the 1916 Lucknow Pact. Over time, as political tensions grew, he emerged as the unshakable voice for Muslim rights, eventually leading the demand for an independent Muslim state.

Role in the Creation of Pakistan

By the 1940s, Jinnah had become the undisputed leader of the All-India Muslim League. His relentless advocacy, constitutional expertise, and powerful oratory transformed the demand for a separate homeland into the reality of Pakistan. On 14 August 1947, Pakistan emerged as an independent nation, fulfilling Jinnah’s vision for a sovereign Muslim state where people of all faiths could live in freedom.

Final Years and Legacy

Despite ill health, Jinnah devoted his last year to setting the foundations of the new nation—emphasizing unity, discipline, and faith. He passed away on 11 September 1948 in Karachi, just over a year after Pakistan’s independence. His enduring legacy as the architect of Pakistan lives on, and he is honored every year on his birthday, 25 December, with tributes across the country.
